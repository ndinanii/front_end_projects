Frantz Fanon

Psychiatrist, Philosopher, Revolutionary

A Brief Overview of His Life and Work

Frantz Omar Fanon (July 20, 1925 – December 6, 1961) was a Martinican-born psychiatrist, philosopher, revolutionary, and writer whose works are influential in the fields of post-colonial studies, critical theory, and Marxism. As an intellectual, Fanon was a political radical, Pan-Africanist, and Marxist humanist concerned with the psychopathology of colonization and the human, social, and cultural consequences of decolonization.

His most famous works include Black Skin, White Masks (1952) and The Wretched of the Earth (1961). These texts explore the psychological effects of colonialism on both the colonizer and the colonized, advocating for the necessity of decolonization, sometimes through violent struggle.

Key Milestones:

  • 1925: Born in Fort-de-France, Martinique.
  • World War II: Fought with the Free French forces.
  • Post-War: Studied medicine and psychiatry in Lyon, France.
  • 1952: Published Peau noire, masques blancs (Black Skin, White Masks).
  • 1953-1956: Served as head of the psychiatry department at Blida-Joinville Hospital in Algeria.
  • Algerian War: Joined the Algerian National Liberation Front (FLN) and became an editor for its newspaper, El Moudjahid.
  • 1959: Published L'An V de la Révolution Algérienne (A Dying Colonialism).
  • 1960: Appointed as ambassador to Ghana by Algeria's provisional government.
  • 1961: Diagnosed with leukemia. Published Les Damnés de la Terre (The Wretched of the Earth) shortly before his death.
  • December 6, 1961: Died in Bethesda, Maryland, USA.
